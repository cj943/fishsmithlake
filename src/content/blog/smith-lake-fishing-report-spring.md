Spring at Lewis Smith Lake arrives gradually but unmistakably. When the redbuds start blooming along the bluffs and the water temperature climbs through the 50s, every species on the lake responds. This is the season the regulars plan their trips around — and for good reason. Here's a general picture of what spring looks like on Smith Lake.

## Water Temperature is Everything

Spring fishing at Smith Lake is governed by water temperature more than any other variable. The lake's depth means it warms slowly — the surface may feel comfortable in early March while the main lake body is still in the low 50s. Fish behavior tracks water temperature closely.

- **48–55°F:** Spotted bass are starting to move from winter deep-water holding areas. Finesse presentations in 15–25 feet on main lake structure start producing.
- **55–62°F:** Prespawn feeding peaks. Spotted bass stack on transition banks and points adjacent to spawning flats. This is often the best bite of the year.
- **62–68°F:** Spawn begins. Bass move shallow — 2–6 feet — over gravel and rock. Crappie also spawn through this range.
- **68°F+:** Post-spawn begins. Fish move off spawning areas but remain relatively catchable as they recover.

Track the water temp at your launch ramp each trip. A thermometer costs $15 and will make you a better angler.

## Spotted Bass: Prespawn is Prime

The prespawn period — typically late March through mid-April — is the best spotted bass fishing of the year at Smith Lake. Fish are concentrated, accessible, and aggressive.

**Where to look:** Transition banks that connect 15-foot flats to 5-foot spawning areas. Rocky points where the bank transitions from deep to shallow. Brushy Creek, Clear Creek coves, and the north arm all have excellent prespawn structure. Fish the depths between where the bass winter (20–30 feet) and where they'll spawn (2–6 feet) — they're in transit, staging, and hungry.

**What works:** Drop shots, shaky heads, and small swimbaits on 6–8 lb fluorocarbon. The clear water demands light line in spring. A small swimbait matched to the shad size — typically a 3-inch paddle tail in ayu or shad colors — is a consistent prespawn producer.

## Crappie: Best Access of the Year

By mid-April, crappie are on their spawn throughout Smith Lake's coves and dock structures. This is the most angler-friendly fishing the lake offers — crappie are shallow, visible, and catchable on simple gear.

Work docks systematically with a 1/16-oz tube jig or a small live minnow under a slip float. Shoot jigs under dock sections into the shaded water adjacent to any structure. A 12-foot jig pole with 6-pound fluorocarbon gives you the reach to place a jig precisely without alerting fish with a cast.

Brushy coves throughout the north arm of the lake — particularly in the Clear Creek and Brushy Creek tributaries — hold excellent crappie spawns. Get there early in the morning before the post-10am boat traffic pushes fish off their beds.

## Stripers in Spring

Spring striper fishing at Smith Lake transitions from the tight, deep winter schools to more spread-out fish following shad into creek arms and tributary mouths. The classic spring striper pattern is trolling or casting large swimbaits near creek mouths and main lake channel transitions.

The biggest striper blowups of fall are over by spring, but schooling activity still happens in the morning, particularly when frontal systems have bass pushed back deep. Watch for bird activity on the main lake in April.

## March: The Mud Season (Sometimes)

Worth noting: March at Smith Lake can include heavy rain events that temporarily reduce clarity, particularly in the creek arms that receive runoff. Even Smith Lake can get somewhat stained after a heavy spring rain. If clarity is reduced, adjust your bait colors — chartreuse and white become more effective when visibility drops. The main lake body typically clears faster than the tributary arms.

## What to Bring in Spring

- Bug spray: The gnats and early mosquitoes arrive before the fish spawn in some years
- Polarized sunglasses: Critical for spotting bass on beds and reading water clarity
- Light rain gear: Spring weather in Alabama changes fast
- Multiple rod setups: A drop shot rod, a swimbait rod, and a topwater rod ready to go covers you for whatever the morning brings
