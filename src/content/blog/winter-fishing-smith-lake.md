Most anglers put the rods away when November turns cold in Alabama. That means Smith Lake is quieter in winter than at any other time of year — and for the anglers willing to layer up and go, it can produce some of the most memorable fishing of the year.

## Why Winter Works at Smith Lake

Smith Lake's exceptional depth is a major factor in winter fishing quality. While shallower lakes see fish completely shut down in cold water, Smith Lake's deepest zones — 60-to-100-plus feet near the dam — maintain relatively stable temperatures throughout the winter. Stripers and large spotted bass use that deep water as a refuge and concentrate there in predictable numbers.

Fewer boats on the water means fish have recovered from fall fishing pressure. A winterized piece of structure that was pounded all fall will hold rested, catchable fish by January.

## Striper Fishing: Winter's Best Show

Winter is the premier striper season at Lewis Smith Lake. From December through February, stripers pack into the deepest water near the dam and main channel, following shad concentrations in 40–80 feet. The fish are cooperative — they'll hit on nearly every drop if you're positioned correctly over the school.

**Technique:** Vertical jigging with blade baits or heavy jigging spoons is the standard approach. Position your boat directly over the school you're seeing on sonar — typically marked as a ball of fish near the bottom in 40–70 feet. Drop a 3/4-oz Silver Buddy or Hopkins jigging spoon to the fish, snap it up 2–3 feet, let it flutter back down. The strike usually happens on the fall.

Use braid (50–65 lb) on your jigging rods for sensitivity and minimal stretch. Tie a 2-foot fluorocarbon leader (20 lb) to the bait for visibility insurance in the clear water.

Cold mornings with calm winds are prime — look for stable weather days rather than frontal conditions for the best action.

## Spotted Bass in Winter

Big spotted bass don't stop feeding in winter — they just slow down and go deep. The fish to target are the lake's largest specimens: 3-to-5-pound spotted bass that have moved from their fall staging areas to main lake ledges in 20–35 feet.

The key is presentation speed. Everything slows down in 50°F water. A shaky head jig that you'd hop aggressively in fall needs to be barely moved in January — crawl it along the bottom, shake it in place, and be patient. A slow-rolling swimbait worked just fast enough to maintain action is another consistent producer.

Football jigs (3/8-to-1/2 oz) worked along deep rocky ledges are a classic winter spotted bass technique at Smith Lake. The heavy head allows precise bottom contact in deep water, and the crawfish profile triggers big bass.

## Reading the Winter Drawdown

Alabama Power typically begins drawing the lake down in late fall, and Smith Lake often sits several feet below full pool through January and February. This changes the presentation of every shoreline feature you fished at full pool.

The good news: drawdown concentrates fish. Less water means fish have less area to roam. Find the remaining water adjacent to structure and you'll find fish. Main lake points, channel edges, and the deepest coves all hold fish more reliably during drawdown than they do at full pool.

## What to Wear

Cold water fishing at Smith Lake in January requires proper preparation:

- **Layered insulation:** Wool or fleece base layers, with wind-resistant outer shell
- **Gloves:** Fingerless fishing gloves or thin gloves that allow lure feel
- **Hand warmers:** Chemical hand warmers in your pockets make a 4-hour winter session bearable
- **Polarized sunglasses:** Still essential — winter sun is low-angle and can be surprisingly blinding on the water
- **Life jacket:** Wear it, especially in winter — cold-water immersion is far more dangerous than warm-water immersion
