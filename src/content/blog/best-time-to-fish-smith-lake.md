Lewis Smith Lake fishes well in every season — but it doesn't fish the same way in every season. The lake's deep, clear water creates distinct seasonal patterns that differ meaningfully from what you'd expect at a shallower, murkier reservoir. Knowing when to go and what to do when you get there is the difference between a great day on the water and a frustrating one.

## Spring: The Prime Season (March–May)

Spring is when Smith Lake is at its most generous. As water temperatures climb from the upper 40s into the mid-60s, spotted bass transition through a predictable prespawn, spawn, and post-spawn pattern that makes them highly catchable.

**March** brings prespawn spotted bass onto the points and transition banks adjacent to spawning flats. Fish are feeding aggressively before the spawn and are catchable on drop shots, shaky heads, and swimbaits in the 8–20 foot range. Finesse gear in 6–8 lb fluorocarbon is the right tool.

**April** is spawn month at Smith Lake, with fish moving into 2–6 feet over gravel and rocky substrate. This is the most accessible spotted bass fishing of the year — fish are visible and predictable.

Simultaneously, crappie fill every shallow cove and dock during the spring spawn in April and early May. A 1/16 oz jig or live minnow under a float in 2–4 feet of water near structure produces consistent action.

The spring prespawn period in March is my personal pick for the single best window on the lake — aggressive spotted bass staging on structure before the spawn are the most cooperative fish of the year.

## Summer: Go Deep, Go Early (June–August)

Summer at Smith Lake is about adaptation. When surface temperatures push into the mid-80s, the fish go deep — spotted bass to 25–40 feet on main lake structure, stripers following shad in the main channel.

The best summer fishing happens between first light and about 10am, when fish are shallow enough to hit topwater lures and the heat hasn't driven them down yet. Topwater poppers and walking baits on main lake points in early morning can be exceptional in June and July.

By midday, go vertical. Drop shot rigs worked slowly over ledges and channel edges in 25–40 feet produce spotted bass throughout the summer. Electronics — good side-imaging and sonar — are essential for finding fish in deep, featureless water.

Night fishing for catfish peaks in summer. Set up on sandy bottom or near a creek channel mouth after dark with cut shad or chicken liver. Summer catfish are active and cooperative.

## Fall: The Exciting Season (September–November)

Fall at Smith Lake produces some of the most exciting fishing moments in freshwater. As water cools, spotted bass go on a feeding binge before winter, and stripers herd shad into creek arms and coves in spectacular surface blowups.

**Topwater fishing** from September through early November is as good as it gets. Schooling spotted bass chasing shad on the surface respond violently to walking baits and poppers. Watch for birds — diving birds in fall mark baitfish schools, and baitfish schools mean stripers.

**Striper blowups** in October and November are the signature experience of Smith Lake fall fishing. Stripers corral shad against banks and surfaces in feeding frenzies that can cover an acre of water. Have an umbrella rig or large swimbait ready.

Fall is also excellent for exploring new spots — fish are less predictable and more spread out than in summer, so covering water with reaction baits pays off.

## Winter: Slow Down and Go Deep (December–February)

Winter gets overlooked by a lot of anglers, but it rewards those who know where to go. Smith Lake in winter offers the best striper fishing of the year and the opportunity for the biggest spotted bass.

**Striper jigging** in the deepest part of the main channel — 40–80 feet near the dam — produces the trophy fish that make Smith Lake famous. Blade baits and jigging spoons worked vertically over suspended fish is the standard technique. It's methodical, cold work. But a 20-pound striper on a December morning is worth it.

**Spotted bass** in winter concentrate on main lake ledges and points in 20–35 feet. Slow finesse presentations — football jigs, deep-running drop shots, and swimbaits worked slowly on the bottom — produce the lake's biggest spotted bass. January and February routinely produce 4-to-5-pound spotted bass at Smith Lake.
